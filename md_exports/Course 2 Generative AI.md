Generative AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that can create new content—text, images, audio, video, code, or data—that resembles human-created content. Unlike traditional AI that classifies or predicts, Generative AI produces original outputs.

Example:

New, original content

## Slide 22

What is Tokenization?

Tokenization

is the process of breaking down text into smaller units called

tokens

that language models can understand and process. Think of it as translating human language into a format the AI can work with.

Key Concept:

LLMs don't read text the way humans do. They convert text into numbers (tokens), process those numbers, and convert back to text.

Why Tokenization Matters

1. Cost Implications

Most LLM APIs charge by token, not by word

Understanding tokens helps you estimate and control costs

Example: "Hello" = 1 token, but "Hello!" might be 2 tokens

2. Context Window Limits

Models have token limits (e.g., 8K, 32K, 128K, 200K tokens)

Your prompt + output must fit within this limit

Exceeding the limit = truncation or errors

3. Performance Impact

More tokens = slower processing

Token efficiency = faster responses

Better prompts use tokens wisely

4. Understanding Model Behavior

Why the model struggles with certain words

Why it's better at English than other languages

Why it can't spell words backward reliably

## Slide 27

What Are Parameters?

Adjustable settings that control how AI models generate responses - like dials on a machine that affect output quality, consistency, and cost.

1. TEMPERATURE: The Creativity Dial

Scale: 0.0 to 1.0

Low Temperature (0.0 - 0.3): Predictable & Consistent

The model gives focused, deterministic answers. Same question = same answer every time.

Medium Temperature (0.5 - 0.7): Balanced - DEFAULT

Good mix of consistency and natural variation. Most common business setting.

High Temperature (0.8 - 1.0): Creative & Varied

Produces diverse, unexpected, creative outputs. More risk of inaccuracy.

## Slide 28

2. CONTEXT WINDOW: The Memory Limit

How much text the AI can "remember" and process at once

Small Context (4K-8K tokens = ~3K-6K words)

Enough for simple Q&A and brief conversations.

Cost:

Low

Medium Context (16K-32K tokens = ~12K-24K words)

Can handle extended conversations and moderate documents.

Cost:

Medium

Large Context (128K-200K tokens = ~96K-150K words)

Can process entire books, large contracts, extensive codebases.

Cost:

High (2-3x more than small context)

Ultra Context (1M+ tokens = ~750K+ words)

Can process multiple documents, entire product documentation, massive codebases.

Cost:

Very high (5-10x more)

Key Decision: Match context size to actual need. Don't pay for 200K context when 8K works fine

## Slide 29

3. MAX TOKENS: Response Length Control

Sets the maximum length of generated response

50-256 tokens (~40-200 words)

Short, direct answers.

Use for:

Classifications, yes/no questions, category labels, simple queries

512-1024 tokens (~400-750 words)

Standard response length.

Use for:

Chat responses, emails, summaries, explanations, basic analysis

2048-4096 tokens (~1,500-3,000 words)

Long-form content.

Use for:

Articles, detailed reports, comprehensive analyses, documentation, complex code

Important: Set max tokens slightly higher than expected need. If too low, responses get cut off mid-sentence. If unnecessarily high, you pay for unused capacity.

## Slide 30

Bonus Parameters (Advanced)

Frequency Penalty (0.0 - 1.0)

Reduces word repetition based on how often words appear. Higher values = less repetition.

Use when:

Generating varied marketing content, avoiding robotic responses

Presence Penalty (0.0 - 1.0)

Encourages introducing new topics/words. Higher values = more diverse content.

Use when:

Brainstorming, exploring different angles

Top-P / Nucleus Sampling (0.0 - 1.0)

Alternative to temperature for controlling randomness.

Note: Use temperature OR top-p, not both. Most businesses should stick with temperature.

Prompt Engineering Techniques:

Zero-Shot Prompting

What is Zero-Shot?

Asking AI to perform a task

without providing any examples

- just direct instructions.

When to Use

✅

Good for:

Simple tasks, common operations, quick queries

❌

Not ideal for:

Complex tasks, domain-specific work, when consistency is critical

Trade-off:

Fast and simple, but less reliable for complex work

## Slide 80

Prompt Engineering Techniques:

Few-Shot Prompting

What is Few-Shot?

Provide

2-5 examples

of the task, then AI follows the pattern.

Principle:

Show, don't just tell.

When to Use

✅

Good for:

Specific formats, brand voice, custom logic, consistency

Impact:

Much more accurate than zero-shot. 3-5 examples is optimal.

## Slide 84

Prompt Engineering Techniques:

Self Consistency Prompting

What is Self-Consistency?

Generate

multiple responses

to the same prompt, then choose the most common or best answer.

Principle:

Wisdom of crowds - if AI gives same answer 8 out of 10 times, it's likely correct.

## Slide 85

Prompt Engineering Techniques:

Self Consistency Prompting

When to Use

✅

Critical for:

High-stakes decisions

When accuracy is paramount

Ambiguous or complex questions

Verification of important outputs

Implementation:

Run same prompt 3-5 times

Compare results

Choose consensus answer or aggregate insights

Trade-off:

Higher accuracy but 3-5x cost and time
